# Tidio

Tidio combines website live chat, automated Flows, and the Lyro AI agent in one customer-communication workspace. It is most useful when a shop or SaaS website receives recurring questions about products, delivery, pricing, or accounts, but still needs people to handle sensitive, unclear, or high-value cases. That makes Tidio a practical front door for a bounded support workflow, not a substitute for a fully governed contact center.

## What Tidio is good for

The visible entry point is the chat widget on a website. Agents can answer in real time, while Flows or Lyro handle first-line questions and collect context before a handoff. For small and mid-sized support or e-commerce teams, that combination is easier to operate than a separate bot experiment because automation is placed where visitors already ask for help.

Tidio is a reasonable fit when the team has a manageable conversation volume, a maintained FAQ, and people available for escalation. It is a weaker fit when the organization needs sophisticated skill routing, deep case management, extensive audit trails, or many regional service teams in one system.

## The components in a real workflow

- **Live Chat and Inbox:** Agents take over when context, empathy, or a business decision is required.
- **Flows:** Rule-based dialogs can cover greetings, qualification, contact capture, and simple routing.
- **Lyro:** The AI agent can use approved sources such as website content, FAQs, manual Q&A, and supported imports. The quality of the source material therefore matters more than a generic promise of automation.
- **Handoff and tickets:** Lyro can be configured to transfer a conversation to agents, keep it in the automated path, or create a ticket when its knowledge is insufficient or a visitor requests a person.
- **Copilot and integrations:** Depending on the setup and plan, Tidio can assist agents and connect the chat workflow with website, commerce, or support systems.

## A safe rollout workflow

Start with real conversations. Sort them by frequency, risk, and desired outcome. Shipping status, opening hours, and product attributes are sensible first topics; refunds, account security, and legal disputes are not good candidates for unsupervised automation.

Clean the FAQ before connecting it. Each answer should have an owner, a source, a validity boundary, and a clear fallback when the information is missing. Then build a small Flow for greeting and qualification, and configure Lyro's tone, allowed topics, and escalation rules. Rules for cancellation, complaints, frustration, or requests for an agent should be explicit rather than left to guesswork.

Test normal questions, ambiguous wording, missing information, and deliberately misleading prompts. Review whether the answer used the right source and avoided inventing a discount, an order status, or a policy. Launch with a restricted channel or audience first, and make sure an agent receives enough conversation context to continue without asking the customer to start over.

## Operations, evaluation, and maintenance

Lyro can learn from website and other approved sources; unanswered conversations and suggested Q&A still require editorial review before activation. This turns the product into an operating routine: inspect failed answers regularly, disable stale content, review escalations, and update sources when products, prices, or policies change.

Track at least resolution without a repeat contact, handoff rate, time to human response, and misrouting rate. A high automation percentage is not a win if customers return with the same problem or agents must correct promises made by the bot. For sensitive topics, an explicit stop-and-handoff path is better than an ambitious answer.

## Integrations and ownership

Tidio can sit on a marketing site or store and connect the conversation to the tools used by the team. Verify each required integration in the current plan and test what is actually synchronized: a link, a contact property, an order context, a ticket, or a full conversation are different capabilities. If knowledge is imported from an existing helpdesk, treat the import as a draft that needs review, not as an automatic publication step.

Assign owners for Flows, Lyro guidance, FAQ sources, escalation queues, and reporting. Without ownership, a chat widget can remain online while its answers become obsolete. Define a fallback channel and an expected response time before advertising “instant” support.

## Privacy, permissions, and governance

Chats may contain names, email addresses, order numbers, and free-text customer details. Before adding the widget, document the purpose, legal basis, retention period, access roles, processor terms, third-party integrations, and deletion process. Ask only for data needed to answer or route the request, and move payment, account recovery, and security decisions into a controlled process.

Restrict who can edit knowledge and escalation rules. A source can be accurate for one customer group and wrong for another, or can become stale after a product change. Review logs and samples with the same care as ordinary support content, and do not use a friendly tone as evidence that an answer is safe.

## Pricing and operating cost

Utildesk lists Tidio under **Freemium**. That label is not a complete budget. Check the provider's current plans, live-conversation allowances, Lyro or Flow quotas, agent seats, channels, integrations, overage rules, and billing period before committing. Also budget for content maintenance, human coverage of handoffs, privacy review, and any connected commerce or helpdesk service. A small subscription can still create a large operational cost if nobody owns the knowledge base.

## Alternatives

- [Intercom](/en/tools/intercom/): A stronger choice for SaaS messaging, help centers, and lifecycle engagement across product and customer journeys.
- [Zendesk](/en/tools/zendesk/): More appropriate for formal ticketing, case management, roles, and support reporting.
- [Freshchat](/en/tools/freshchat/): A natural option when chat should stay close to other Freshworks service workflows.
- [LiveChat](/en/tools/livechat/): A more focused website live-chat option when human conversations matter more than a broader AI automation layer.
- [Crisp](/en/tools/crisp/): A leaner messaging approach for smaller teams that want shared conversations with less setup overhead.

## Editorial Assessment

Tidio is recommended for small and mid-sized website or shop teams with recurring questions, maintained support content, and real human coverage for escalation. It creates value when it reduces repeat contacts and speeds up handoff, not merely when a bot is switched on. Organizations with regulated, highly complex, or ticket-centric support should usually start with Zendesk or another specialized service platform instead.

## FAQ

**Can Tidio replace human support completely?**

No. Flows and Lyro can absorb recurring questions, but missing knowledge, exceptions, and sensitive decisions need a defined human route. Test that route with real conversations before launch.

**How can a team reduce incorrect Lyro answers?**

Limit sources to approved content, write explicit escalation guidance, and review suggested answers from unanswered conversations before enabling them. Monitor stale content, misrouting, repeat contacts, and samples of successful as well as failed chats.

**Is Tidio suitable for an online store?**

It can be, especially for recurring product, delivery, and order questions. The store still needs a secure human process for refunds, account data, exceptions, and any action that the current integration does not reliably support.

**What should be decided before installing the widget?**

Define privacy and retention requirements, support roles, response targets, escalation channels, content ownership, integration boundaries, and the current cost model. Then run a limited pilot with quality measures rather than exposing every visitor immediately.
