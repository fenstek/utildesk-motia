# Adept

Adept positions itself as a provider of agentic AI that does not just generate text, but is meant to understand software interfaces and carry out work steps in existing tools. Instead of classic chatbot responses, the core idea is this: a user describes a goal, and an AI agent plans the necessary steps across websites, applications, documents, or internal systems.

That makes Adept especially interesting for companies that have many manual process steps in mature software environments. At the same time, it is not a typical self-service tool for quick personal use. Anyone evaluating Adept should think more in terms of use cases, data access, security approvals, and process control than in terms of individual prompts.

## Who is Adept for?

Adept is a good fit above all for organizations that want to embed AI agents into real workflows:

- Operations, finance, supply chain, or back office teams with recurring software processes
- Companies that want to automate internal workflows across multiple web apps, PDFs, forms, and systems
- Product and innovation teams evaluating agentic AI as an infrastructure component
- IT and security teams that need to think about automation not only through APIs, but also through user interfaces
- Business teams that want to keep human control in the process, for example for final approvals or sensitive decisions

Adept is less suitable for simple text generation, small one-off automations, or users who immediately expect a no-code tool with ready-made app triggers. For that, ChatGPT, Zapier, Make, or Power Automate are often more direct.

## Typical use cases

- **Analyze documents and update systems:** Extract information from PDFs or contracts, check it, and then transfer it into internal applications.
- **Research across multiple portals:** Gather availability, delivery information, or status data from different websites.
- **Support back office processes:** Prepare recurring work steps while critical actions are confirmed by people.
- **Test enterprise agents:** Check whether an agentic approach helps where API integrations would be too expensive, too slow, or incomplete.
- **Make process knowledge visible:** Translate repeatable workflows into natural language and document them in a way that business teams can understand more easily.

## Core features

- **Agentic process execution:** User goals are translated into multi-step actions instead of being returned only as text.
- **Web and UI understanding:** Adept focuses on recognizing and operating elements in software interfaces.
- **Document and data understanding:** Websites, PDFs, tables, and forms can all be part of a workflow.
- **Multi-step planning:** Complex tasks are broken down into subtasks and worked through.
- **Feedback loops:** Corrections and human feedback can help make workflows more reliable.
- **Enterprise orientation:** Governance, approvals, roles, and data security are central evaluation points for productive use.
- **Human-in-the-loop:** Especially for sensitive workflows, Adept should be understood as assistance with control, not as a blind autopilot.

## Workflow fit: when Adept makes sense

The best fit is for processes that recur often but are not cleanly automatable through APIs. These can include legacy web portals, internal tools, PDF-heavy workflows, or multi-step review processes. Adept is interesting where classic RPA is too rigid and pure chatbots are too passive.

Adept is less suitable when a process is already well automated via API or when the task does not have stable repeatability. For creative one-off tasks, content drafts, or simple spreadsheet automations, broader AI or automation tools are usually quicker to get started with.

## Pros and cons

### Pros

- Strong focus on real software actions instead of text output alone
- Interesting for enterprise workflows that are difficult to automate
- Natural language can bring business teams closer to automation
- Human-in-the-loop approaches fit well with controlled processes
- Can fill gaps when API integrations are missing or too costly

### Cons

- Less immediately tangible for small teams than classic no-code automation
- Productive use requires detailed process analysis, testing, and security approvals
- Agentic workflows need clear boundaries, logging, and escalation rules
- Pricing, availability, and specific implementation models should be checked directly with the provider
- Not every UI-based task is stable enough for reliable automation

## Privacy & Data Notes

Adept potentially touches sensitive company data because agents can act across applications, websites, documents, and internal systems. Before a pilot project, companies should clarify exactly which data is processed, where logs are created, which systems are connected, and what actions an agent is allowed to take on its own.

For productive workflows, a staged model is recommended: first observe and suggest, then automate partial tasks, and later allow only clearly limited actions. Critical steps such as submissions, contract changes, or decisions involving personal data should remain subject to human confirmation.

## Pricing & Costs

The specific pricing and access structure should be checked directly on the official website or through sales. Since Adept is strongly oriented toward enterprise workflows, implementation, security review, pilot phase, internal support, and ongoing monitoring are relevant in addition to the pure tool price.

## Alternatives to Adept

- **UiPath:** Stronger in the classic RPA space with structured enterprise processes.
- **Microsoft Power Automate:** A good choice when a company already works deeply within the Microsoft ecosystem.
- **Zapier or Make:** Faster for app-to-app automations with clear triggers and actions.
- **OpenAI API or ChatGPT Enterprise:** More flexible for custom AI assistance, text work, and integrated agent logic.
- **Automation Anywhere:** An enterprise RPA alternative for structured automation and governance.

## Editorial assessment

Adept should not be classified as just another AI writing tool, but as a building block for agentic automation. Its appeal lies in the fact that software can be operated the way people operate it. That is also where the responsibility lies: without clear boundaries, testing, and data rules, an agent can do more harm than a passive assistant. For mature teams, Adept is therefore an interesting candidate; for spontaneous individual tasks, less so.

## FAQ

**Is Adept a chatbot?**

Not in the classic sense. The focus is more on agents that translate intentions into actions across software interfaces and workflows.

**Can Adept replace APIs?**

Not as a general rule. If a stable API is available, it is often more robust. Adept is especially interesting when processes run through interfaces, documents, or multiple systems that are not cleanly integrated.

**Which tasks are suitable for a pilot?**

Good candidates are frequent, rule-based workflows with clear inputs, visible intermediate steps, and low risk in case of errors.

**Do you need developers for Adept?**

For a demo, not always. For productive enterprise processes, however, IT, security, and process owners are important.

**How do you evaluate the quality of an agent?**

Not just by a successful demo. Repeatability, error cases, logging, permissions management, and clear stop conditions matter.

**Is Adept suitable for sensitive data?**

That depends on the contract, architecture, and internal policies. Before productive use, privacy, access concepts, and logging should be reviewed.

**When is Zapier the better choice?**

If the only goal is to move data between known SaaS apps according to fixed rules, Zapier is often faster to set up.

**What is the most important starting point?**

A small, well-bounded workflow with clear success measurement. Agentic AI should not start with the most critical process.
