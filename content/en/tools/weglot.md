# Weglot

Weglot makes an existing website multilingual without building a separate site for every language. It detects published content, creates translations, serves language versions on their own URLs, and gives teams a place to correct and approve the result. That makes it a website-localisation operating layer, not merely a translation box.

The quick first impression can be misleading. A machine-translated page is not automatically an international-ready page. Terminology, prices, legal text, metadata, product names, forms, and support routes all need rules of their own. Weglot can handle technical delivery; the publisher still owns the content.

## Who is Weglot for?

Weglot is particularly useful to marketing, ecommerce, and web teams that want to test or operate a live website in a small number of additional languages. It is practical for WordPress, Shopify, Webflow, Wix, and similar sites, and can also work with custom front ends when there is a clear integration path.

It is less suitable when every locale is effectively a separate product, with a different catalogue, editorial team, and content system. In that situation, deliberately separate CMS and localisation workflows may be easier to govern.

## What it handles in day-to-day work

- **Content detection and synchronisation:** New or changed website text can enter the translation workflow instead of being tracked manually in spreadsheets.
- **Machine translation with editing:** Teams can review and correct draft translations in a dashboard or visual editor.
- **Terminology protection:** Glossaries and rules keep brand, product, and specialist terms from being retranslated inconsistently.
- **Language-specific delivery:** Weglot supports language directories or subdomains, a language switcher, translated metadata, and `hreflang`.
- **Team workflow:** Editors, translators, and marketers can work on the same translations without direct access to the CMS.
- **Dynamic website content:** The product is designed to cover common web and commerce integrations, but critical pages still need hands-on testing.

## A sensible rollout

Start with one language and a valuable but limited page set: the home page, three core landing pages, a conversion page, and key navigation and error messages. Before enabling it, decide which pages must never be auto-translated, who approves legal content, and which terms belong in the glossary.

After go-live, technical and editorial checks belong together. Does every public page have an appropriate language version? Do canonicals and `hreflang` make sense? Are titles, descriptions, forms, emails, and checkout messages consistent? Expand to the full estate only after those checks. A language switcher should help visitors, not override their conscious choice with unwanted redirects.

## Editorial Assessment

Weglot is a credible shortcut when the aim is to publish an existing website in more languages quickly, technically cleanly, and with a manageable editing loop. Its distinctive value is the combination of delivery, translation management, visual editing, and SEO support - not the idea that raw machine output is automatically ready for market.

We recommend it for a clearly owned launch with a few languages and a measurable international goal. Exercise caution around regulated content, complex pricing or contract logic, and markets with substantially different editorial needs. In those cases, review, approval, and a fallback path must be designed into the workflow.

## SEO, cost, and control

Language-specific URLs, translated metadata, and `hreflang` are helpful foundations, not an automatic guarantee of international visibility. Each locale needs real user value, relevant search intent, and clean internal linking. An unreviewed translation can be indexable while still harming brand trust or search quality.

Pricing depends on the plan and the amount of translatable content. Before signing, count what the product will actually detect: pages, dynamic elements, product variants, archived content, and future languages. Also establish where translations live, how they can be exported, and what a migration would involve if the architecture changes.

## Alternatives

- [DeepL](/en/tools/deepl/) is a strong complement or alternative when high-quality text translation and a self-managed integration are the main requirements.
- [Lokalise](/en/tools/lokalise/) is more focused on product localisation, software strings, translation projects, and developer workflows.
- [Crowdin](/en/tools/crowdin/) is an option for teams coordinating localisation across many contributors, content types, and releases.
- [Phrase](/en/tools/phrase/) is aimed at larger localisation programmes that need central translation management and governance.

## FAQ

**Does every Weglot translation need manual review?**

Not every low-risk UI string needs the same effort. Pages involving prices, legal claims, brand voice, conversion, or specialist advice should have an accountable review and explicit approval criteria.

**Is Weglot automatically good for international SEO?**

It makes important technical foundations such as language URLs, metadata, and `hreflang` easier. Rankings still depend on intent, content quality, and correct implementation in the particular site.

**How can we avoid incorrect specialist terms?**

Use a glossary, tone examples, and review the highest-value page types. Turn recurring corrections into rules rather than fixing the same term page by page.

**What should be tested before launch?**

Test navigation, indexability, forms, price and legal text, mobile rendering, redirects, and the route back to the source language. A small pilot exposes these mistakes much more cheaply than launching ten languages at once.
