# MailerLite

MailerLite is an email marketing platform for newsletters, signup forms, landing pages, websites, and automated campaigns. It is a practical fit for small businesses, creators, associations, and shops that want a repeatable sending process without introducing a full CRM first. The important boundary is that MailerLite does not replace consent design, list ownership, or human review of campaign content.

## Who is MailerLite for?

MailerLite makes sense when one person or a small marketing team needs to collect contacts, design messages, organise audiences, and run a modest email journey. Typical users include newsletter publishers, local businesses, small e-commerce teams, and organisations with recurring updates. Teams looking for complex account structures, deep sales processes, or an enterprise customer-data platform should compare it with a more specialised CRM or marketing automation system.

## What does MailerLite cover?

The platform connects several steps of a basic email process. Signup forms and landing pages bring new contacts into an audience; groups and segments turn that audience into usable targets; and the newsletter editor prepares campaigns for desktop and mobile. Reports then provide signals such as clicks, unsubscribes, and campaign engagement for the next editorial decision.

Visual automations use triggers, delays, conditions, and actions. A welcome sequence can begin after signup, a click can send a subscriber down another path, and a webhook can pass data to an external system. This is useful while the workflow has one clear purpose and someone checks failed steps, stale groups, accidental duplicates, and outdated messages.

## How should a first rollout work?

A useful pilot does not require a large migration. Start with one outcome, such as a welcome series for a download or a monthly customer newsletter. Check the form, consent wording, sender identity, unsubscribe path, and data fields before designing the emails. Only after test contacts reach the intended group should the team activate automation.

Every workflow needs an entry condition, an expected result, an owner, and a documented manual fallback. Test sends should cover links, personalisation, mobile rendering, delays, exclusions, and duplicate entry. After the first real send, do not judge the setup by opens alone: click quality, unsubscribes, complaints, workflow errors, and maintenance effort reveal whether the process is actually working.

## What are the practical strengths?

- **Low entry barrier:** The editor, forms, and visual workflows are approachable for small teams that do not need an enterprise suite.
- **Connected workflow:** Lists, landing pages, campaigns, and basic automation can be maintained in one product.
- **Useful targeting:** Groups, fields, and segments support more relevant sending when the underlying data is maintained carefully.
- **Measurable iteration:** Reports and A/B testing can inform editorial decisions, although neither replaces a meaningful test design.

## Where are the limits?

MailerLite cannot make sending successful by itself. Deliverability still depends on consent, sender reputation, content, list hygiene, and an appropriate cadence. Complex CRM relationships, granular permissions, or many dependent lifecycle paths may become difficult to govern. An automation that is technically active can still be outdated or misleading for subscribers.

Before rollout, define who owns segments, templates, and automations; how bounces and unsubscribes are handled; and when inactive contacts are removed. An export and shutdown plan also matters. It prevents a growing audience from becoming an expensive or difficult-to-replace dependency.

## Privacy and data responsibility

Email addresses, interests, clicks, purchases, and segment membership can be personal data. MailerLite provides features and legal documents relevant to privacy work, but a claim of GDPR support is not automatic compliance. The organisation still needs to assess purpose, legal basis, processing terms, retention, deletion, integrations, and access rights.

Use a traceable signup process, separate consent for genuinely different purposes, and limited data sharing with connected services. Do not use real sensitive contacts as test data. Before production sending, check sender identity, required legal information, unsubscribe handling, and the process for complaints.

## Pricing and total cost

MailerLite uses a freemium model. At the time of this review, the provider lists the Free plan for up to 250 active subscribers and 2,500 emails per month; once a free account exceeds its limit, campaigns and automations are paused. MailerLite changed free-plan limits in 2026, so the [official pricing page](https://www.mailerlite.com/pricing) should be checked before purchase.

The real cost is more than the subscription: audience size, sending volume, extra seats, domains, integrations, consent operations, editorial maintenance, and migration effort all matter. A paid rollout should also check plan limits, billing behaviour when the audience grows, and the cost of moving cleanly to another system later.

## Alternatives

- [Mailchimp](/en/tools/mailchimp/): a good comparison when a familiar ecosystem and broader marketing features matter more than the leanest workflow.
- [Brevo](/en/tools/brevo/): worth considering when email needs to share a channel mix with SMS or transactional messaging.
- [ConvertKit](/en/tools/convertkit/): more focused on creators, newsletter businesses, and digital products.
- [ActiveCampaign](/en/tools/activecampaign/): a stronger candidate when deep automation and CRM-adjacent sales processes are central.
- [GetResponse](/en/tools/getresponse/): useful to compare when email sits inside wider campaign, webinar, or funnel work.

## Editorial Assessment

MailerLite is a sensible choice for small and mid-sized teams with a clear newsletter or lead-nurturing process: form, consent, segment, campaign, and bounded automation. Its advantage declines when the team does not maintain its data logic or when an existing CRM already owns complex customer processes. Run a real pilot before committing, and measure whether it saves time without weakening deliverability, privacy, or team handoffs.

## FAQ

**Is MailerLite suitable for a first newsletter?**

Yes, when the list is small, the sending cadence is clear, and one person owns the process. Start with real but limited contacts, verified consent, and a short review after the first send.

**What happens if a free account exceeds 250 active subscribers?**

According to the current provider information, campaigns and automations are paused on the Free plan until the account upgrades or returns below the limit. Recheck the live limit and enforcement before launch because plan rules can change.

**Can MailerLite automate a welcome sequence?**

Yes. A trigger such as joining a group can start a sequence, while delays, conditions, and actions control the path. Test exclusions, repeat entry, and a manual fallback before activation.

**Does MailerLite automatically make a programme GDPR-compliant?**

No. The service offers relevant controls and documents, but the organisation remains responsible for consent, purpose limitation, contracts, deletion, integrations, and access governance.
